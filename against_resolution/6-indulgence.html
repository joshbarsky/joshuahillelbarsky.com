VI – On Self-Indulgence, Exposure, and the Economy of Attention

To write from the self in the current cultural climate is to incur suspicion. The label of self-indulgence operates as both accusation and containment. It signals a perceived excess — a failure to modulate interiority, a collapse of aesthetic restraint, a breach of the unspoken contract between writer and audience. Yet the term itself remains conceptually underdeveloped. It polices tone, quantity, and affect, without defining a stable criterion for judgment.

Self-indulgence is commonly understood as the unchecked expression of personal experience, rendered without sufficient modulation, transformation, or justification. It implies an excess of interiority that refuses to translate into shared insight. In this sense, it is not the presence of the self that provokes resistance, but its density, its refusal to be stylized or made productive.

Yet the cultural conditions surrounding this notion have shifted. We now inhabit a digital economy in which confession, performance, and visibility are not only normalized, but incentivized. The self is no longer hidden; it is optimized. Platforms reward intimacy, stylized affect, and the simulation of sincerity. In this context, the charge of self-indulgence loses its critical edge — it becomes indistinguishable from the medium itself.

This saturation produces a paradox. The hunger to be seen is exacerbated by the flood of simultaneous attempts at recognition. The more visible one becomes, the more anonymous. Expression collapses into redundancy. The need for validation folds in on itself, creating a recursive vacuum — a condition in which the desire to be affirmed is consumed by its own ubiquity. Presence, once meaningful, becomes generic.

The essays accompanying Manhood operate within this collapse. They do not seek distance or resolution. They document a condition from within its event horizon. The use of AI as a critical mirror heightens this recursive quality — not by clarifying the author’s position, but by exposing the structure of its repetitions. These texts are not expository, but symptomatic. Their movement is not from question to answer, but from saturation to residue.

What is often identified as self-indulgence in this context is not an act of narcissism, but of proximity. It is the refusal to extract meaning from pain, the refusal to convert lived disintegration into digestible form. Rather than polishing experience into clarity, these essays preserve its ambiguity, its failure to resolve. The discomfort they produce arises not from what they say, but from what they withhold: resolution, mastery, transcendence.

That discomfort is not incidental; it is cultural. To say this is not to trivialize the unease, but to contextualize it. Culture, here, refers to the dominant structures of value, attention, and interpretation. These structures prize legibility, relatability, and closure — qualities that make a work marketable, discussable, affirming. To transgress these expectations is not to be obscure, but to refuse the grammar of value itself. What feels self-indulgent is often what does not yield, what does not respond, what refuses the circulation of digestible meaning.

Such refusal is not without consequence. It challenges the aesthetic contract. It denies the reader catharsis. It rejects the consolations of form. But this is not a violation; it is an ethic. To remain near suffering without stylizing it into content is not a weakness. It is a gesture of fidelity.

To write in this register is also to confront the nausea of inevitable narcissism. Rather than disavow the impulse to be seen, or dress it in the language of humility, the text permits the condition to emerge in its raw, synthetic form — an act that mirrors the broader generational landscape. In the current cultural logic, sincerity is both currency and costume. Self-exposure has become a genre. What was once considered indulgent is now ubiquitous — but diluted. The peak of Maslow’s hierarchy, once associated with transcendence or realization, now threatens to soften into a post-authentic gelatin. The result is a strange convergence: a culture that demands exposure, yet resents its effects.

In the current economy of attention, where performative authenticity circulates as capital, the refusal to package interiority becomes the more radical gesture. These essays, like the book they accompany, resist catharsis. They do not offer a narrative of healing. They offer persistence.

To dwell in one’s own ruins, without resolution or clarity, is not a claim to depth or singularity. It is an index of condition. The gesture is neither heroic nor novel. It is simply unhidden. And in its refusal to be shaped into clarity, it leaves behind a trace — not a lesson, not an argument, but a form of presence that resists being consumed.
