Emotional Containment and Metaphysical Decay

In Manhood, the recurring symbol of the floating orb becomes a site of emotional, bodily, and metaphysical inquiry. What begins as a surreal moment of comic tension — a floating orb of congealed lard and psychic frustration — unfolds into a vast network of metaphor that binds domestic life to ancient rituals, waste to inheritance, and bodily excretion to spiritual continuity. This essay traces the tupperware motif through the book, illustrating how repression, storage, and delayed confrontation give rise to both sickness and philosophical insight.

The first instance of the floating orb introduces a grotesque externalization of the narrator’s inner life: “A tangled warm beige, pink orb of galvanized lard.” It is both comic and repulsive, likened to a jellyfish, a salt lamp, or a fatberg. The orb is not just a floating mess; it is accumulated mental sediment — a psychic abscess formed by domestic stress and existential overload. The narrator’s theatrical attempt to puncture it with a swiss army knife is less about humor and more of a symbolic urge to purge. It is not just a blob, it is a wound.

Yet this act is interrupted. Miriam enters, holding the child, and gently says, “Please don’t.” She offers not a solution but an alternative: containment over catharsis. Her gesture — handing the narrator a tupperware container to store the blob — marks the emergence of a central motif. Emotion, instead of being expressed or expelled, is stored. The domestic becomes the metaphorical: tupperware, a banal vessel, becomes an archive of existential sludge.

We learn the tupperware remains in the fridge, untouched. What was meant to be stored “for later” is not processed but neglected. The narrator’s coping mechanism — containment — becomes pathology. This rot mirrors his writing: each time he pauses from work, “the sadness… began to flood back, slowly like foam.” Foam is a telling metaphor — formless, ungraspable, light but suffocating. It coats everything.

The narrator’s response is compulsive labor. Yet he admits its futility: “no matter how much I worked… it was never enough.” When he stops, sadness seeps in. Standing before the moat of his feelings, he realizes: “there was not enough tupperware for it all.” The vessel of repression is overwhelmed.

Small acts of bodily restoration follow: pressing his palm to his head, trimming his beard, clipping nails. These gestures are humble but vital. They are rituals of control, of re-entry into the self. The narrator then returns to his notebook and writes. Unlike earlier scenes, this writing is not salvation. But it is movement. The spiral continues.

Staring into the mirror, the narrator admits, “the mirror was the mirror.” The line is tautological, but significant: the self is what it is, unchangeable. He reflects on his dissatisfaction with the previous chapter, but something shifts. “To see the value of that discomfort. The traction it created.” This is a philosophical turn — discomfort is not an obstacle but a generative tension. It recalls Adorno’s negative dialectics: contradiction as a path to truth.

The tupperware remains in the fridge: “congealed and rotten and apart.” This is the cost of delay. Repression has spoiled. The earlier blob has decayed. Yet it is also a moment of realization. The narrator sees the limits of storage. The symbolic ecosystem completes:

The blob: emotional surplus. The tupperware: containment. The fridge: the subconscious. The rot: deferred confrontation.

When he finally opens it, the consequence is immediate: vomiting. The confrontation is too late. Emotion becomes physical illness. The narrator spends the night “rotting in the bandwidth,” passively consuming YouTube. Even his creative thoughts are “lopsided and meaningless.” Writing, once a vessel, is now infected. The scene dramatizes how unprocessed grief poisons both body and mind.

The narrator describes being behind an “abscess moat, of congealed tupperware muck.” It is a grotesque image — his repression has built a fortress of sludge. Yet within it, “a glint.” A flicker of selfhood. Sovereignty, however compromised. He is “alone in [his] own reality,” which is both tragic and empowering. This isolation becomes a kind of aesthetic position: damaged, quarantined, but real.

Eventually, the narrator opens the fridge “somewhat mindlessly.” Miriam says she threw things out. The tupperware is on the drying rack: grease-stained, wet, leaning. Not discarded. Not restored. Just there. The repression has been rinsed, but not erased. Memory lingers. So does the mess.

The book then expands into a metaphysical taxonomy: the four humors. Blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile. Each linked to temperament, season, and disease. The narrator describes digestion as the site of spiritual and creative meaning: “a good humor could not be made from bad chylous.” If writing is digestion, then illness — imbalance — corrupts both the body and the text.

Semen is described as the fifth fluid, sacred, the culmination of perfect balance. This introduces the long passage of Genesis 17 — the covenant with Abraham. Circumcision becomes both literal and symbolic: a cut that binds lineage, obedience, and male identity.

This Biblical recitation is followed by a livestream of a bris. But the synagogue is empty. A man enters, fiddles with a book, then leaves. The sacred has become procedural. The ritual is congealed tupperware: unattended, recorded, lifeless. And yet, the narrator watches.

In a museum, the narrator sees an iron sculpture. He feels annoyed by the rust. He compares it to his cast iron pan at home. Maintenance becomes a metaphor for care, both external and internal.

He stops at McDonald’s. Orders a small coffee. This is, he jokes, “his character arc.” But it is sincere. Restraint is a kind of growth. He sits at a table, eats a value burger, loses at chess, and looks at strangers. He feels, finally, ordinary.

But then he recalls the sculpture: “its foreskin seemed to envelop its body entirely.” Circumcision again. The default form denied to him. His own cut flesh thrown away. His manhood reduced to waste. The sculpture is no longer inert — it is a mirror.

He watches a new object float — another blob, born of sex, of chance, of covenant. But it is not just life or death. It is something in between: “a fractured milk glow.” The book ends not with clarity, but with motion. “The object fell then, towards its maximum velocity.” This final line returns to the book’s first: terminal velocity. The culmination of gravity, grief, ejaculation, and fall.

Manhood does not resolve its metaphysical crises. It lets them rot, until the reader can see them clearly. The tupperware is not salvation. The blob is not expunged. The notebook is used, but only sometimes. The narrator offers a vision of manhood that is ruptured, fragmented, and nauseated — but also oddly sacred. Its sacredness lies not in covenant. Not in writing a perfect chapter. Not in belief, but in presence.

Manhood teaches that grief and rage can be stored, but not forever. They leak. They spoil. They demand form. And in the absence of God, of balance, of purpose, what remains is the will to endure.
